The Ecosystem Equation: Why Perth's Innovation Hub Works — and What Most Cities Get Wrong

Brodie McCulloch · Feb 2026 · 10 min read

In 2012, we started Spacecubed in a single room in Perth's CBD. No government grant. No university backing. No corporate sponsor. Just a co-founder, a conviction, and a credit card.

Thirteen years later, Spacecubed operates across 7 locations, has supported thousands of founders, hosted hundreds of events, and become the connective tissue of Western Australia's innovation ecosystem. The Plus Eight Accelerator has deployed $3.7M, seen $24M+ in follow-on capital, and built a $150M+ portfolio. She Codes has upskilled 6,000+ women in technology.

People ask me all the time: how did you do it?

"The answer isn't about the space. It was never about the space."

What Actually Makes an Ecosystem

Most cities trying to build innovation hubs make the same mistake: they confuse the outputs for the inputs. They build the building before they build the community. They announce the precinct before they understand the market. They hire the consultants before they talk to the founders.

An ecosystem is, at its core, a set of relationships and norms that allow resources — capital, talent, knowledge, opportunity — to flow efficiently between people who need them and people who have them. The building is just where people bump into each other. The program is just an excuse to be in the same room.

The Three Things That Actually Matter

1. Trust infrastructure. Before you can have deals, you need trust. Before trust, you need repeated interaction. This is why coworking works when it's designed right — it creates the casual, low-stakes interactions that compound into something real over time. VGW, which went on to be valued at $3 billion, met key people at a Spacecubed breakfast. Not at a formal pitch event. Breakfast.

2. Genuine tolerance for failure. Silicon Valley's secret weapon isn't the money — it's the social permission to fail. In Perth in 2012, failing publicly was career-ending. We worked hard to shift that norm: celebrating companies that tried and pivoted, amplifying stories of resilience, creating structures where failure didn't destroy the founder's reputation or network.

3. Patient capital and patient institution-building. Ecosystems take 10-15 years to mature. Most government programs run on 3-year funding cycles. Most corporate innovation initiatives last 18 months before the sponsor moves on. The single biggest structural problem in ecosystem building is the mismatch between the timeline of institutional support and the timeline of ecosystem development.

The Churchill Fellowship Lesson

When I did my Churchill Fellowship research in 2014, I visited 12 countries studying innovation ecosystems. I went expecting to find the secret formula. What I found instead was that every successful ecosystem had a slightly different formula — but they all shared one trait: they had built around genuine, local strengths, not imported templates.

Perth's strength is proximity to Asia, deep resource-industry expertise, and a culture of practical problem-solving. Our ecosystem works when we lean into those strengths. It fails when we try to be Silicon Valley.

What I'd Tell My 2012 Self

Build slower. The instinct when building an ecosystem is to grow fast — more events, more members, more programs, more square metres. But depth beats breadth every time. Ten founders who are deeply embedded in your community and trust each other are worth more than a thousand people with access cards.

And trust the compounding. The connections that happen in year one pay dividends in year five. The board member who joins your program in year two introduces you to the investor who funds your most important company in year seven. Ecosystems are long games. Play them that way.
